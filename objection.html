The plot included a sizeable car park that comfortably accommodated the hotelâ€™s guests. However, with part of this space earmarked for development, parking was set to become a significant concern in the area. Babbacombe already faced seasonal parking pressures, particularly during the busy summer months when visitor numbers surged. Although the plans included provisions for a number of new parking spaces, it was clear that the available capacity would fall short of ideal levels, raising fears of increased congestion on nearby streets.

Parking, however, was just one of several objections raised against the redevelopment. When details of the proposed plans were circulated, a total of 37 formal comments were submitted, all opposing the project. Among the most vocal opponents were residents of Homecombe House, a retirement complex built in 1984. Many of them expressed concerns that the new buildings would block natural light from reaching their apartments, while others feared a loss of privacy due to direct overlooking of their windows.

Additional worries were voiced over emergency access. With increased parking pressure expected, some residents argued that vital emergency services might struggle to navigate the area efficiently. Furthermore, the demolition and construction process itself was seen as a major disruption. Locals feared prolonged noise, dust, and traffic congestion, which could affect not only nearby residents but also businesses and visitors. Despite these objections, the development was approved, signaling a major transformation for the site and the surrounding community.
